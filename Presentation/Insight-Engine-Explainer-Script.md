# Insight Engine — Explainer Video Script

*Narration to run over `Insight-Engine-Explainer.pptx` (10 slides). ~2 min 45 s at a calm ~140 words/min. Each block is also in the matching slide's speaker notes. Tone: plain, accurate, unhurried — no hype.*

---

**Slide 1 — Title**
> This is the Insight Engine — a tool for analysis you can actually defend. Its premise is simple: the large language model provides the insight; the engine makes that insight verified, defensible, and audience-ready.

**Slide 2 — The problem**
> Some decisions don't forgive a confident guess — high-stakes, complex, or wicked problems, where the important things hide, the evidence is mixed or interested, and the deciding questions are value judgments. A strong model will hand you a fluent answer. But fluent isn't the same as verified — or defensible.

**Slide 3 — The idea**
> So the engine doesn't try to be smarter. It tries to be more honest. It takes the model's insight and wraps it in a layer of assurance: verify what can be verified, grade how strongly each claim stands, separate fact from value — and make you own the value calls.

**Slide 4 — How it works**
> Here's what happens when you hand it a problem. It right-sizes the effort, surfaces what sits where attention doesn't go, verifies each load-bearing claim, and — for genuinely systemic problems — maps the feedback structure. Then it separates the questions no evidence can settle, locks its grades, and stops at the one step that's yours.

**Slide 5 — Grade-lock**
> Every load-bearing claim is marked: V if it's independently corroborated, N if it rests on an interested party — with strength tiers on top. And once a grade is set, nothing downstream can change it; it can only be re-expressed. That's what makes the analysis hold up after the fact.

**Slide 6 — Disconfirmation**
> It also tries to prove itself wrong. For each claim the conclusion rests on, it runs a second search aimed at the opposite — looking for the evidence that would break it. That's the direct counter to confirmation bias, the failure mode most AI research tools quietly share.

**Slide 7 — The forced gate**
> And there's one moment the engine refuses to take from you. The questions no evidence can settle — the framing, the legitimacy, the trade-offs — are handed back to you, and the analysis won't finalise until you take a position. Testing showed that merely showing you these questions doesn't work; being made to answer them does.

**Slide 8 — Four commands**
> It's four commands. Analyse runs the whole pipeline. Verify fact-checks a set of claims. Render re-voices a finished analysis for a specific reader — without changing a grade. And track keeps an investigation live as new facts arrive.

**Slide 9 — ICD-203**
> None of this is invented from scratch. The engine is architected around the nine analytic tradecraft standards the U.S. intelligence community holds its analysts to — and in a blind assessment it scored ninety-two percent against a strong model's seventy-five. To be honest about it: that's architected-around and assessed once — not a formal certification.

**Slide 10 — Close**
> The engine is finished being built; now it's for using. It's an open plugin for Claude Code and Cowork. Hand it a real problem, let the gate make you take a position — and get back an analysis you can defend.
